GOVERNMENT AND POLITICS IN ISRAEL

Explore Israel's dynamic government and political landscape, from its parliamentary system and democratic processes to the key leaders and policies shaping the nation today.

ISRAEL PRESIDENT: Isaac Herzog

Born: September 22, 1960 (age 65 years), Tel Aviv-Yafo, Israel

Previous offices: Leader of the Opposition in Israel (2013–2018)

Spouse: Michal Herzog (m. 1985)

Party: Israeli Labor Party

Education: Cornell University, Ramaz Lower School, Tel Aviv University, New York University

Parents: Chaim Herzog, Aura Herzog

Isaac "Bougie" Herzog is an Israeli politician who has been serving since 2021 as the president of Israel. He is the first president to have been born in Israel after its Declaration of Independence.

The President of Israel serves as the head of state, a position that is largely ceremonial and apolitical. Unlike the Prime Minister, who holds executive power and leads the government, the President acts as a unifying figure and a moral compass for the nation.

Core Branches of Government

  • Legislative (Knesset): A unicameral body of 120 members elected every four years. It is responsible for passing laws, approving the budget, and electing the President.
    • Executive (Government): Led by the Prime Minister (currently Benjamin Netanyahu), who serves as the head of government. The President (currently Isaac Herzog) is the head of state, but the role is largely ceremonial.
  • Judicial (Courts): An independent branch topped by the Supreme Court, which also functions as the High Court of Justice, providing a check on the executive and legislature.

The Electoral System

  • Proportional Representation: Voters choose a party list rather than an individual candidate.
  • Electoral Threshold: To enter the Knesset, a party must receive at least 3.25% of the national vote.
  • Frequent Elections: While terms are meant to be four years, coalitions often collapse early; between 2019 and 2022, Israel held five national elections.

Current Political Landscape (2025–2026)

Government Composition: The current governing coalition is widely considered the most right-wing and religious in Israel's history, including parties such as Likud, Shas, United Torah Judaism, and Otzma Yehudit.

Legal Framework

Israel does not have a formal written constitution. Instead, it is governed by a series of Basic Laws that carry constitutional weight and define the country’s fundamental institutions and rights.

Regional & External Conflicts

  1. The Gaza War (War of Redemption): Following the October 7, 2023, attacks, Israel launched a massive offensive in Gaza. Although a U.S.-brokered ceasefire was formally established on October 10, 2025, sporadic fighting continues. In early February 2026, Israel conducted extensive airstrikes in response to what it called "blatant violations" of the truce by Hamas.
  2. Conflict with Hezbollah (Lebanon): After a major escalation in 2024, a 60-day ceasefire took effect in late November 2024. However, tensions remain extremely high; in early 2026, the IDF continues thwarting operations to prevent Hezbollah's rearmament, while Hezbollah remains ambiguous about its involvement in any future confrontation.
  3. Tensions with Iran: The long-standing proxy war shifted toward direct confrontation in 2024 and 2025. By February 2026, analysts have noted the looming prospect of a major conflict between the U.S., Israel, and Iran, which could distract from domestic peace efforts.
  4. Syrian & Regional Spillover: The fall of the Assad regime in Syria (facilitated by the weakening of its allies, Hezbollah and Iran) has created new security vacuums. Israel continues to operate in Syria to prevent Iranian-backed groups from establishing a foothold.
